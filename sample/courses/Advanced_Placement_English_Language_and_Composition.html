Advanced Placement English Language and Composition

Course Overview

This Advanced Placement English Language and Composition course is a college-level program focused on developing students' abilities to analyze rhetoric and compose effective arguments. Students will engage in the close reading of a wide variety of complex non-fiction texts from various periods and disciplines, learning to identify and analyze rhetorical strategies, stylistic choices, and modes of persuasion. The course emphasizes the development of skills in argumentative, analytical, and synthesis writing, preparing students thoroughly for the AP English Language and Composition exam.

Key Texts & Rhetorical Concepts

Rhetorical Analysis

Understanding and identifying ethos, pathos, logos; analyzing style, tone, syntax, diction, figurative language, and overall authorial purpose in texts by authors like Swift, Orwell, King Jr., Tan, Woolf, and Gladwell.

Argumentation

Constructing and evaluating arguments; identifying claims, evidence, warrants, assumptions, and counterarguments; recognizing logical fallacies. Texts include foundational documents like The Declaration of Independence and influential speeches.

Synthesis of Information

Integrating information from multiple sources to form a coherent, well-supported argument; evaluating sources for credibility and relevance; citing sources appropriately.

Analysis of Various Non-Fiction Genres

Engaging with essays, journalism, political writing, speeches, historical documents, scientific articles, and visual rhetoric (e.g., political cartoons, advertisements) to understand how arguments are crafted across different media.

Style and Voice

Developing an awareness of one's own writing style and voice, and learning to adapt style for different audiences and purposes. Focus on clarity, precision, and engaging prose.

Vocabulary for Rhetoric & Argument

Building a strong vocabulary specific to rhetorical analysis and argumentation (e.g., anaphora, parallelism, antithesis, syllogism, inductive/deductive reasoning).

Educational Approach

Close Reading of Complex Texts

Intensive practice in reading and annotating challenging non-fiction texts to understand content, purpose, audience, and rhetorical strategies. Emphasis on identifying claims, evidence, and stylistic choices.

Essay Writing & Revision

Regular practice in writing timed and process-based essays (rhetorical analysis, argument, synthesis). Emphasis on developing clear theses, organizing arguments logically, integrating evidence effectively, and revising for clarity and impact through peer and teacher feedback.

AP Exam Preparation Strategies

Targeted instruction and practice for all sections of the AP exam, including multiple-choice questions analyzing rhetorical elements and free-response essays. Strategies for time management and effective outlining are covered.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into units focusing on key rhetorical concepts, argumentation skills, and essay types required for the AP exam. Each unit involves the analysis of diverse non-fiction texts (essays, speeches, articles, historical documents), discussions on rhetorical strategies, and practice writing assignments. Vocabulary related to rhetoric and argumentation is taught explicitly and reinforced. Students will complete multiple full-length practice essays and receive detailed feedback. The course aims to build student confidence and proficiency in analyzing and constructing sophisticated arguments in preparation for college-level academic work.
