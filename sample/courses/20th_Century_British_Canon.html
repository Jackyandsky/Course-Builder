20th Century British Canon

Course Overview

This course delves into six significant works from the 20th-century British literary canon, offering students an opportunity to analyze complex narratives and thematic concerns. Through authors like E.M. Forster, Virginia Woolf, Aldous Huxley, Samuel Beckett, Kazuo Ishiguro, and Doris Lessing, students will explore topics such as social class, modernism, dystopian societies, existentialism, memory, and metafiction. The curriculum is designed to enhance critical reading, analytical essay writing, and vocabulary skills.

Educational Approach

Thematic and Stylistic Analysis

Students will analyze key themes such as British social structures (Forster), modernist consciousness and time (Woolf), dystopian futures (Huxley), absurdism (Beckett), memory and identity (Ishiguro), and metafiction/feminism (Lessing) using close reading and critical discussion.

5/5/5 Analytical Essay Writing

The course emphasizes structured analytical writing through the 5/5/5 essay format, focusing on developing strong thesis statements, using textual evidence effectively, and crafting coherent arguments on various literary aspects of the selected works.

LEX Vocabulary and Contextual Study

Systematic vocabulary building through the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) is integrated with the study of literary concepts, historical contexts (e.g., early 20th-century British society, post-war anxieties), and critical essays on British literature.

Curriculum Structure

This 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected canonical works. Each unit includes reading approximately 100-125 pages per week, regular LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering all letters A-Z cumulatively through the course), and detailed class discussions centered on "Questions to Ponder" for each text. Students will complete three separate 5/5/5 analytical essays for each work, exploring aspects like character analysis, thematic interpretation, and comparison with other literary pieces or philosophical ideas (e.g., comparing Huxley with Orwell, analyzing Beckett's absurdism). The course also incorporates poetry from the late-Victorian/early-20th century and critical essays on British literature and relevant authors.
