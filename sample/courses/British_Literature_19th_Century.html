19th Century British Canon

Course Overview

This course provides an intensive study of six influential works from the 19th-century British literary canon. Students will engage with celebrated authors such as Jane Austen, Mary Shelley, Charlotte Brontë, Emily Brontë, Charles Dickens, and Oscar Wilde. The curriculum focuses on analyzing the rich tapestry of Victorian society, the rise of industrialization, Romantic sensibilities, evolving gender roles, social reform movements, and the complexities of the British Empire. Emphasis is placed on critical reading, thematic exploration, character analysis, and the development of sophisticated analytical essays using the 5/5/5 method, supported by LEX vocabulary enrichment.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Victorian Society & Literary Movements

Exploration of key themes reflecting 19th-century British life: social class and mobility (Austen, Dickens), Romanticism and the Gothic (Shelley, Brontës), individualism and morality (Brontës, Wilde), and the impact of industrialization and empire.

Thematic and Character-Driven Essays (5/5/5 Method)

Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays for each work, focusing on themes such as marriage and society, scientific ambition, female agency, passionate love, social critique, and aestheticism, alongside in-depth character analyses.

LEX Vocabulary and Historical Context

Comprehensive vocabulary development (LEX Letters A-Z) integrated with discussions on the historical and cultural backdrop of 19th-century Britain, including Victorian values, social reform efforts, and philosophical currents like utilitarianism and aestheticism.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around five-week intensives for each of the six selected canonical works. Each unit involves focused reading (100-125 pages per week), regular LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering letters A-Z cumulatively), and in-depth class discussions based on "Questions to Ponder." Students will complete multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, analyzing character development, central conflicts, symbolism, and authorial purpose. The course incorporates supplementary readings, including critical essays on 19th-century British literature, poetry of the era, and relevant historical documents to enrich understanding.
