20th Century American Canon

Course Overview

This course offers an intensive exploration of six seminal works recognized as part of the 20th Century American literary canon. Students will engage in deep analysis of novels and plays by authors such as Ken Kesey, William Faulkner, Tennessee Williams, Richard Wright, Alice Walker, and Bret Easton Ellis. The curriculum focuses on understanding complex themes, character motivations, narrative structures, and the socio-historical contexts that shaped these influential pieces of American literature, developing critical reading and analytical writing skills.

Educational Approach

In-Depth Character and Thematic Analysis

Focuses on detailed character studies (e.g., McMurphy, Joe Christmas, Stanley Kowalski, Bigger Thomas, Celie, Patrick Bateman) and exploration of core themes such as mental health, racial conflict, gender dynamics, violence, consumerism, and the reliability of narrative.

Structured Analytical Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Utilizes the 5/5/5 essay format for critical responses, emphasizing clear thesis development, strong textual evidence, and sophisticated argumentation to address complex literary questions and character motivations.

Contextual and Critical Readings

Integrates supplementary materials including critical essays on American literature, analyses of race and gender representation (e.g., Jim Crow era, images of Black women), and discussions on literary concepts like the tragic hero and unreliable narration. LEX Vocabulary (A-Z) is incorporated throughout.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around five-week intensives for each of the six selected canonical works. Each intensive involves focused reading (100-125 pages per week), LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering letters A-Z throughout the course), and detailed class discussions based on "Questions to Ponder." Students will complete multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, exploring characterization, thematic elements (e.g., conflict, evolution, didactic purpose), and literary techniques. The course also includes samples and critical essays on American literature and relevant historical periods (e.g., the American South, the Jim Crow era, 20th-century images of race and prejudice).
