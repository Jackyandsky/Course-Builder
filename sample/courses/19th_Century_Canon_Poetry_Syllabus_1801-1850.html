19th Century Canon Poetry (1801-1850 Focus)

Course Overview

This course offers a focused exploration of canonical poetry from the first half of the 19th century (1801-1850), a period rich with Romanticism and burgeoning Victorian sensibilities. Students will engage with influential poets of this era through thematic intensives, analyzing how they addressed themes of the individual versus society, war, love, mortality, personal growth, and the evolving societal landscape. The course emphasizes deep poetic analysis, understanding of literary devices, and contextualizing works within their specific historical and cultural moments of the early to mid-19th century.

Thematic Explorations & Featured Poets (1801-1850 Emphasis)

Individual Versus Society (Early 19th C.)

Wordsworth (The World is Too Much With Us), Whitman (Early Leaves of Grass excerpts), Longfellow (Early Song of Hiawatha excerpts), C. Rossetti (Early works), Shelley (Prometheus Unbound - related ideas), Beaudelaire (Early influences)

War (Napoleonic & Post-Napoleonic Era)

Shelley (Misery’s Sharpest Fang), Tennyson (Early war poems predating Charge of the Light Brigade if applicable), Melville (Early influences for war poetry)

Romantic Love & Early Victorian Ideals

Keats (Ode to a Grecian Urn, Sonnets), Poe (Annabel Lee), E.B. Browning (Sonnets from the Portuguese context), Bradstreet (as precursor), Donne (as precursor), R. Browning (Early works like Porphyria’s Lover)

Death, Loss, and Survival (Romantic & Early Victorian)

Dickinson (Because I Could Not Stop), Keats (When I Have Fears), Shelley (Ozymandias), Poe, R. Browning (Parting at Morning), Emerson (The Snow-Storm)

Coming of Age and Growth (Romantic Era)

Coleridge (Kubla Khan), Shelley (Ode to the West Wind, To A Skylark), Keats (To Autumn), Blake (The Lamb), Poe (The Happiest Day, the Happiest Hour)

Early Stirrings of Modernity

Emerson (Brahma), early works of R. Browning and E.B. Browning reflecting societal shifts.

Educational Approach

Focused Thematic Analysis (1801-1850)

Students engage in deep thematic analysis of poems primarily from the first half of the 19th century, writing 5/5/5 essay responses weekly. The course includes comparative analysis of poems within this specific period.

Introduction to Poetic Devices

Progressive introduction to and application of poetic devices and concepts relevant to Romantic and early Victorian poetry, enhancing students' analytical toolkit. Includes critical essays and literary analysis exercises.

LEX Vocabulary Enrichment

Comprehensive vocabulary development through the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z), with regular quizzes to reinforce learning and ensure application in written analyses of period-specific poetry.

Curriculum Structure

This 30-session course is organized into six five-week thematic intensives, with a specific focus on poets and poems prominent between 1801 and 1850. Themes include: Individual Versus Society, War, Love, Death/Loss/Survival, Coming of Age/Growth, and Early Modernity. Each intensive explores relevant poems from the period. Students will read 1-3 poems per week, complete 5/5/5 essay responses, and engage with critical literary analysis. LEX vocabulary is integrated throughout. The course culminates in a university-quality in-depth analysis unit essay on a poem from the 1801-1850 period.
