Shakespeare: An Overview

Course Overview

This course offers an intensive overview of the dramatic and poetic works of William Shakespeare, the most celebrated writer in the English language. Students will explore a range of his major tragedies, comedies, histories (selected), and sonnets, delving into his unparalleled use of language, profound characterizations, and enduring themes of love, ambition, jealousy, fate, and the human condition. The curriculum emphasizes close reading of Shakespearean English, understanding Elizabethan theatrical conventions, analyzing dramatic structure, and developing sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) supported by LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Shakespearean Language & Poetics

Close reading and interpretation of Shakespeare's blank verse, prose, soliloquies, and figurative language (metaphors, similes, puns). Understanding iambic pentameter and the nuances of Elizabethan English.

Dramatic Structure & Character Archetypes

Exploration of Shakespeare's mastery of dramatic structure (five-act plays), development of complex characters (tragic heroes, comic figures, villains), and use of theatrical conventions of the Globe Theatre.

5/5/5 Analytical Essays & LEX Advanced Vocabulary

Development of advanced analytical writing skills through 5/5/5 essays on themes, character motivations, dramatic irony, and poetic imagery. Comprehensive vocabulary building with LEX Advanced (Letters A-Z) and Shakespearean terms.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around intensive study of Shakespeare's major works, typically dedicating 2-4 weeks to each selected play, with specific sessions for his sonnets. Each unit involves close reading of the text, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and detailed class discussions on "Questions to Ponder" related to plot, character psychology, themes, and historical context (Elizabethan England). Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per play. The course includes primers on the Elizabethan worldview, the conventions of Renaissance drama, and critical perspectives on Shakespeare's enduring legacy.
