Advanced Placement U.S. History

Course Overview

This Advanced Placement U.S. History course offers a college-level exploration of American history from pre-Columbian societies to the present. Students will analyze the development of American identity, political systems, economic structures, social dynamics, and culture. The course emphasizes critical thinking, the interpretation of historical evidence from primary and secondary sources, and the ability to construct well-supported historical arguments. The curriculum is designed to prepare students thoroughly for all components of the AP U.S. History exam.

Key Historical Periods & Thematic Focus

Period 1-3 (1491-1800)

Pre-Columbian societies, European colonization, development of colonial America, the American Revolution, and the establishment of the early republic. Focus on interactions, governance, and identity.

Period 4-5 (1800-1877)

The Antebellum Era, westward expansion, growing sectionalism, the Civil War, and Reconstruction. Focus on democracy, slavery, reform movements, and national unity.

Period 6-7 (1865-1945)

The Gilded Age, industrialization, immigration, Progressivism, American imperialism, World War I, the Great Depression, and World War II. Focus on economic change, social reform, and America's role in the world.

Period 8-9 (1945-Present)

The Cold War, the Civil Rights Movement, social and political transformations of the late 20th century, and contemporary American society. Focus on foreign policy, domestic issues, and globalization.

American and National Identity (NAT)

Exploring how ideas about American identity and values have developed and changed over time, and how these ideas have been contested.

Politics and Power (POL)

Analyzing the development of political institutions, beliefs, and practices, and their impact on American society and government.

Educational Approach

Historical Thinking Skills Development

Focus on mastering skills such as analyzing historical causation, patterns of continuity and change over time (CCOT), comparison, contextualization, and crafting historical arguments using evidence.

Interpretation of Diverse Sources

Regular practice in analyzing a wide range of primary sources (letters, diaries, speeches, government documents, political cartoons) and secondary sources (historians' interpretations) to understand multiple perspectives.

AP Exam Essay Preparation (DBQ, LEQ, SAQ)

Intensive instruction and practice in writing Document-Based Questions (DBQs), Long Essay Questions (LEQs), and Short Answer Questions (SAQs), including thesis development, evidence integration, and understanding scoring rubrics.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured chronologically across nine historical periods, as defined by the AP U.S. History curriculum framework. Each unit involves lectures, class discussions, rigorous analysis of assigned readings (e.g., from Kennedy's "The American Pageant" or Foner's "Give Me Liberty!"), and engagement with a variety of primary and secondary sources. Students will complete numerous practice essays (DBQs, LEQs, SAQs) and multiple-choice question sets to prepare for the AP exam. The course aims to provide a thorough understanding of American history and develop the analytical skills necessary for success in college-level history courses.
