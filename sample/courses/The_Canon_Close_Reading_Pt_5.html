The Canon: Close Reading Part V - 17th Century & Early Enlightenment

Course Overview

This fifth course in "The Canon: Close Reading" series explores the rich literary landscape of the 17th century and the dawn of the Enlightenment. Students will engage in intensive textual analysis of works by seminal authors like Milton, Molière, Racine, Pascal, Bunyan, and Behn, examining themes of religious and political upheaval, scientific discovery, philosophical inquiry, neoclassical aesthetics, and the emergence of new literary forms. The curriculum emphasizes advanced close reading, understanding complex historical and intellectual contexts, and crafting sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) supported by LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Baroque & Neoclassical Literature

Close reading of texts to understand the characteristics of Baroque literature (e.g., Milton's grand style) and Neoclassicism (e.g., Molière's comedies of manners, Racine's tragedies), including their engagement with classical forms and contemporary issues.

Exploration of Religious, Philosophical, and Scientific Ideas

Understanding how literature of this period reflects and engages with the religious debates of the Reformation and Counter-Reformation, the philosophical inquiries of thinkers like Pascal, and the impact of the Scientific Revolution.

Advanced Interpretive Essays (5/5/5 Method)

Crafting well-argued analytical essays that explore thematic complexities (e.g., free will, reason vs. passion, social satire, early colonialism), character psychology, and literary techniques. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected authors/works (or substantial excerpts). Each unit involves rigorous close reading of assigned texts, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on literary style, thematic interpretations, and the intellectual currents of the 17th century and early Enlightenment. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, developing their ability to articulate sophisticated literary arguments. The course aims to provide a deep understanding of this pivotal period in Western literature and thought.
