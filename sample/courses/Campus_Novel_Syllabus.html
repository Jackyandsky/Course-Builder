Advanced Reading: The Campus Novel

Course Overview

This advanced course explores the distinctive genre of the campus novel, examining its relevance, the societal and academic critiques it offers, and its evolution in the modern era. Students will engage with a variety of campus novels, analyzing how authors depict academic culture, intellectual life, student-faculty dynamics, and the university as a microcosm of broader societal issues. The course focuses on developing critical thinking and sophisticated essay writing skills through discussions, analysis of related articles, and structured writing assignments using the 5/5/5 method and LEX vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Thematic Analysis of Academia in Literature

Critical examination of how campus novels satirize, critique, and reflect academic life, educational philosophies, intellectualism, and societal issues through literary analysis and discussion of related articles from publications like The New Yorker and University Affairs Magazine.

Structured Essay Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Development of structured analytical writing using the 5/5/5 essay format, focusing on argumentation, textual evidence, thematic exploration (e.g., comedy of manners, necessity of the campus novel), and adherence to format, with peer and teacher feedback.

LEX Vocabulary Enrichment & Quizzes

Systematic vocabulary building using the LEX curriculum (e.g., 20 words from LEX Letter A weekly, progressing through A-Z) and words from texts, reinforced by regular quizzes and application in written and oral discussions.

Curriculum Structure

This 30-session course explores ten key campus novels, from Mary McCarthy's "The Groves of Academe" to Donna Tartt's "The Secret History". Each novel is typically studied over three weeks. Weekly activities include reading assignments (approximately 70-100 pages), LEX vocabulary quizzes, novel review and topic discussions (e.g., 'comedy of manners', 'necessity of the campus novel'), and essay response assignments (opinion/argumentative or 5/5/5 essays). Students also engage with supplementary articles and primary source documents where relevant. The course culminates in a final essay tying together themes from two novels read, addressing the question: "Is the campus novel simply a comedy of manners, or does it serve a higher purpose?"
