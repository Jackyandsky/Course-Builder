The European Novel: 20th Century

Course Overview

This course examines the diverse and transformative landscape of the 20th-century European novel. Students will explore how authors such as Proust, Kafka, Mann, Camus, Grass, and Kundera responded to the profound societal, political, and philosophical shifts of the era, including two World Wars, the rise of totalitarianism, existentialism, and postmodern thought. The curriculum emphasizes close reading of complex narratives, analysis of modernist and postmodernist techniques, understanding of historical and intellectual contexts, and the crafting of sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) with LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Modernist and Postmodernist Innovations

Close reading to identify and analyze key techniques of 20th-century European fiction, such as stream of consciousness, narrative fragmentation, allegory, existential themes, unreliable narrators, and intertextuality.

The Novel's Response to Historical Trauma and Ideology

Exploring how European novelists grappled with the impact of world wars, fascism, communism, the Holocaust, and the Cold War, examining themes of alienation, memory, identity, and the search for meaning in a fractured world.

Advanced Critical Interpretation & Argumentation (5/5/5 Essays)

Crafting insightful analytical essays that interpret complex literary works, engage with philosophical and critical ideas, and articulate sophisticated arguments about style, theme, and socio-historical significance. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the selected major authors/works (or substantial excerpts), with some works serving as review or contextual touchstones from previous Canon courses. Each unit involves rigorous close reading, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on narrative strategies, philosophical questions, and the intellectual and cultural currents of 20th-century Europe. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, developing their capacity to analyze and critique challenging modern European literature. The course aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of the key literary developments and enduring masterpieces of this era.
