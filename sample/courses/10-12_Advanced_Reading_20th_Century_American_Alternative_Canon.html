Level 10-12 Advanced Reading: 20th Century American Alternative Canon

Course Overview

This advanced course for levels 10-12 explores an "alternative canon" of 20th-century American literature, moving beyond traditional selections to engage with a diverse range of voices, genres, and themes. Students will analyze works that challenge conventional narratives and reflect varied American experiences, focusing on critical thinking, sophisticated literary analysis, and vocabulary enrichment through in-depth study of novels, plays, and supplementary texts.

Educational Approach

Diverse Thematic Exploration

Engages with a wide array of themes including class, mental illness (Fitzgerald, Plath, Jackson), queer identity (Baldwin), addiction (O'Neill), race and beauty standards (Morrison), genre fiction (King, Dick), and postmodern critiques of society (DeLillo, Ellis).

Critical Literary Analysis & 5/5/5 Essays

Development of advanced analytical skills through in-class discussions, response questions, and structured 5/5/5 essays focusing on character development, narrative techniques (e.g., unreliable narrators), and thematic significance.

Canon Reconstruction & Contextual Reading

Encourages students to question and reconstruct the literary canon by analyzing "alternative" texts alongside supplementary articles (e.g., from The New Yorker, Harvard Literary Review) and short stories (e.g., Poe, Kincaid, Salinger).

Curriculum Structure

This 30-session course is structured around the intensive study of eleven novels and plays, typically dedicating 2-3 weeks per major work. Weekly activities include LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering Letters A-Z), in-depth novel discussions, analysis of specific literary concepts (e.g., Fitzgerald's themes, unreliable narrators, the Bildungsroman, Gothic horror, queer literature, postmodernism, existentialism), and engagement with critical articles and short stories. Writing assignments include 300-400 word analytical responses and more formal 5/5/5 essays. The course includes an in-class essay on challenging the American canon and culminates with a comparative analysis final essay. A reading of David Foster Wallace's "This is Water" concludes the course.
