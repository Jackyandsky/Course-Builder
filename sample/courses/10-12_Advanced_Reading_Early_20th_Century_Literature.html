Level 10-12 Advanced Reading: Early 20th Century Literature (1900-1950)

Course Overview

This advanced course for levels 10-12 surveys influential English language literature from 1900 to 1950, a period marked by profound societal shifts, global conflicts, and literary modernism. Students will analyze how authors like Steinbeck, Hurston, Fitzgerald, Orwell, Kafka, Joyce, Wharton, Beckett, and Camus captured the zeitgeist through themes of social protest, love and isolation, race and identity, war and its aftermath, existentialism, and the absurd. The course focuses on developing critical interpretation, analytical essay writing, and an advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Historical and Philosophical Contextualization

Examines literature within its historical context (e.g., The Great Depression, World Wars, rise of Fascism and Communism) and in relation to influential philosophical movements like Existentialism and Absurdism.

Rigorous Essay Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Development of analytical and argumentative skills through 5/5/5 essays focusing on character analysis, thematic interpretation (e.g., social protest, love, identity, satire, Kafkaesque elements), and literary allegory.

Comprehensive Vocabulary & Poetic Analysis

Systematic vocabulary building with the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) and analysis of early 20th-century poetry (e.g., Langston Hughes, T.S. Eliot, W.B. Yeats) to understand poetic devices and thematic expressions of the era.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session program explores ten pivotal literary works from 1900-1950, allocating approximately three weeks per text. Each unit involves reading assignments (100-125 pages weekly), LEX vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth discussions of the works' themes, characters, and historical significance. Students will complete 5/5/5 essays for each major text, along with analyses of related short stories (e.g., by Guy de Maupassant, Flannery O'Connor, Faulkner), poetry packages (Hughes, Eliot, Stevens, Neruda etc.), and critical articles (e.g., Orwell's "Politics and the English Language"). Key literary concepts such as symbolism, allegory, the Bildungsroman, Kuntslerroman, tragicomedy, and the Kafkaesque are explored. The course culminates in a final essay based on a chosen work or comparative theme, and a reflective discussion on David Foster Wallace's "This is Water."
