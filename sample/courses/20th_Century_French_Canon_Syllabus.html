20th Century French Canon

Course Overview

This course explores significant works from the 20th-century French literary canon, offering an intensive study of authors like Saint-Exupéry, Camus, Proust, Céline, Sagan, and Houellebecq. Students will analyze themes of existentialism, the human condition, love and relationships, memory and time, societal critique, and the evolution of French literature. The curriculum focuses on developing advanced critical reading, analytical essay writing using the 5/5/5 method, and comprehensive vocabulary skills through the LEX program.

Educational Approach

Philosophical and Thematic Analysis

Engages with core philosophical ideas such as existentialism (Camus, Sartre primer) and absurdism, alongside literary themes like memory and recollection (Proust), societal critique (Céline, Houellebecq), and the complexities of human relationships (Sagan).

5/5/5 Structured Essay Writing

Students will hone their analytical writing abilities by composing 5/5/5 essays for each major work, focusing on character analysis, thematic interpretation (e.g., love, symbolism, narrative structure), and the authors' unique contributions to French literature.

Comprehensive Vocabulary (LEX A-Z)

Systematic vocabulary acquisition through the complete LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z), with regular quizzes to enhance students' capacity for nuanced literary discussion and written analysis.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected French canonical works. Each unit includes focused reading (approximately 100-125 pages per week), LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering all letters A-Z cumulatively), and in-depth class discussions based on "Questions to Ponder" for each text. Students will complete multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays exploring character, theme, and literary concepts like symbolism, narrative voice, and frame narratives. The course integrates primers on existentialism (including Camus' "Myth of Sisyphus"), discussions on French literary traditions like romanticism, and critical essays on French literature and its cultural impact.
