19th Century American Canon

Course Overview

This course provides an intensive study of six foundational works from the 19th-century American literary canon. Students will engage with authors such as Hawthorne, Melville, Thoreau, Whitman, Douglass, and Twain, exploring key themes that shaped American identity: individualism, democracy, nature, social reform, the frontier experience, and the complexities of race and freedom. The curriculum emphasizes critical reading, thematic analysis, character development, and the mastery of analytical essay writing through the 5/5/5 method, supported by LEX vocabulary enrichment.

Educational Approach

Analysis of American Literary Movements

Exploration of key 19th-century American literary movements including Romanticism, Transcendentalism (Thoreau, Whitman, Emerson primer), Realism, and the development of unique American voices and narrative styles.

Thematic and Character-Driven Essays (5/5/5 Method)

Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays for each work, focusing on themes such as sin and guilt (Hawthorne), obsession and the sublime (Melville), individualism and nature (Thoreau), democracy and selfhood (Whitman), slavery and freedom (Douglass), and societal critique (Twain).

LEX Vocabulary and Historical Context

Comprehensive vocabulary development (LEX Letters A-Z) integrated with discussions on the historical and cultural context of 19th-century America, including issues of abolition, westward expansion, and social reform movements.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around five-week intensives for each of the six selected canonical works. Each unit involves focused reading (100-125 pages per week), regular LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering letters A-Z cumulatively), and in-depth class discussions based on "Questions to Ponder." Students will complete multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, analyzing character evolution, central conflicts, symbolism, and authorial purpose. The course incorporates supplementary readings, including critical essays on 19th-century American literature and relevant historical documents to deepen understanding.
