Advanced Placement World History: Modern

Course Overview

This Advanced Placement World History: Modern course offers a college-level exploration of global history from c. 1200 C.E. to the present. Students will investigate significant events, individuals, developments, and processes across different regions and time periods. The course focuses on developing historical thinking skills such as analyzing primary and secondary sources, making historical comparisons, understanding causation, and identifying patterns of continuity and change over time. The curriculum is designed to prepare students thoroughly for the AP World History: Modern exam by covering key concepts through thematic and chronological approaches.

Key Historical Periods & Thematic Focus

Unit 1-2: The Global Tapestry & Networks of Exchange (c. 1200 - c. 1450)

Developments in Dar al-Islam, state-building in Africa and Asia, belief systems, consequences of connectivity (e.g., Silk Roads, Mongol Empire, Indian Ocean trade).

Unit 3-4: Land-Based Empires & Transoceanic Interconnections (c. 1450 - c. 1750)

Empires (e.g., Ottoman, Mughal, Safavid, Ming/Qing, Russian), European exploration, Columbian Exchange, maritime empires, challenges to state power.

Unit 5-6: Revolutions & Consequences of Industrialization (c. 1750 - c. 1900)

The Enlightenment, nationalism and revolutions (American, French, Haitian), the Industrial Revolution, industrialization's impact on society, imperialism, and migration.

Unit 7-9: Global Conflict, Cold War, Decolonization & Globalization (c. 1900 - Present)

Shifting power after 1900, World War I & II, global economic crisis, Cold War, spread of communism, decolonization, and contemporary global challenges and advancements.

Humans and the Environment (ENV)

Analyzing how human societies have shaped and been shaped by their environments and climates over time.

Governance (GOV) & Economic Systems (ECN)

Examining various forms of state formation, political ideologies, economic systems, and their transformations across global history.

Educational Approach

Mastery of Historical Thinking Skills

Focus on developing and applying historical thinking skills: developments and processes, sourcing and situation, claims and evidence in sources, contextualization, making connections (comparison, causation, CCOT), and argumentation.

Analysis of Diverse Historical Sources

Regular practice in analyzing a wide array of primary and secondary sources, including written documents, images, maps, charts, and scholarly interpretations, to understand diverse perspectives and construct historical arguments.

AP Exam Skill Development (DBQ, LEQ, SAQ)

Targeted instruction and practice in writing Document-Based Questions (DBQs), Long Essay Questions (LEQs), and Short Answer Questions (SAQs), focusing on thesis construction, evidence integration, and adherence to AP scoring guidelines. Multiple-choice strategies are also emphasized.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around the nine units of the AP World History: Modern curriculum framework, exploring history chronologically and thematically. Each unit involves lectures, engaging discussions, close analysis of historical sources (using texts like Strayer's "Ways of the World" or Bentley's "Traditions & Encounters"), and AP-style practice questions. Students will complete multiple DBQs, LEQs, and SAQs, receiving comprehensive feedback to refine their historical analysis and writing. The course aims to provide a deep understanding of modern world history and thorough preparation for all aspects of the AP exam.
