The Canon: Close Reading Part IV - Shakespeare & His Contemporaries

Course Overview

This fourth course in "The Canon: Close Reading" series centers on the unparalleled genius of William Shakespeare and the vibrant literary scene of his contemporaries during the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras. Students will engage in intensive textual analysis of selected Shakespearean plays (tragedy, comedy, history/romance) and works by other influential dramatists and poets like Ben Jonson and John Donne. The curriculum emphasizes understanding dramatic conventions, poetic language, character complexity, and thematic depth, while honing advanced analytical essay writing (5/5/5 method) and LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Shakespearean Drama & Poetics

Intensive close reading of Shakespeare's verse and prose, soliloquies, dramatic irony, and character development. Understanding the conventions of the Elizabethan stage and Shakespeare's theatrical innovations.

Comparative Study of Contemporaries

Examining works by Shakespeare's contemporaries like Jonson and Donne to understand the broader literary landscape, including city comedy, metaphysical poetry, and differing dramatic styles.

Advanced Literary Argumentation (5/5/5 Essays)

Crafting sophisticated analytical essays that explore complex themes (e.g., appearance vs. reality, order vs. chaos, nature of evil), character motivations, and the richness of poetic language. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each major author/work or grouping (e.g., a Shakespearean tragedy, a comedy, works of Jonson/Donne). Each unit involves meticulous close reading of assigned texts, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on literary techniques, critical interpretations, and historical context (e.g., Renaissance worldview, patronage, censorship). Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per unit, developing their capacity for sustained literary argumentation. The course aims to foster a profound appreciation for the literary achievements of Shakespeare and his era.
