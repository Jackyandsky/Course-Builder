The Canon: Close Reading Part VI - The Age of Enlightenment

Course Overview

This sixth course in "The Canon: Close Reading" series focuses on the literature of the Age of Enlightenment (roughly the 18th century). Students will engage in intensive textual analysis of influential works by authors such as Swift, Pope, Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, and Fielding. The curriculum explores themes of reason, satire, social critique, individualism, the rise of the novel, and philosophical inquiry that characterized this transformative period. Advanced close reading skills, contextual understanding, and the crafting of sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) with LEX Advanced vocabulary are central to the course.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Enlightenment Thought and Satire

Close reading of texts to understand core Enlightenment ideals (reason, progress, natural rights) and the prevalent use of satire to critique societal norms, institutions, and human folly.

The Rise of the Novel and New Literary Forms

Examining the development of the novel as a major literary form during this period, alongside other genres like philosophical tales, essays, and poetic satire. Analyzing narrative techniques and authorial voice.

Advanced Analytical Argumentation (5/5/5 Essays)

Crafting insightful and well-supported analytical essays that engage with the philosophical ideas, social commentary, and literary innovations of Enlightenment texts. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected Enlightenment authors/works (or substantial excerpts). Each unit involves rigorous close reading of assigned texts, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on literary style, thematic interpretations, and the intellectual and cultural currents of the 18th century. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, honing their ability to articulate complex arguments with clarity and precision. The course aims to provide a profound understanding of this pivotal era in literary and intellectual history.
