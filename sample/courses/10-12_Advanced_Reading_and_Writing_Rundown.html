Level 10-12 Advanced Reading and Writing Foundational Course

Course Overview

This comprehensive course for levels 10-12 is designed to enhance advanced reading comprehension, critical analysis, and sophisticated writing skills. Students will engage with a diverse range of literary genres, including novels, short stories, and plays, focusing on understanding complex themes such as magical realism, satire, the Bildungsroman, existentialism, and societal critique. The curriculum emphasizes rigorous vocabulary development and the application of analytical frameworks to produce well-structured and insightful essays.

Educational Approach

5/5/5 Essay Method

A structured writing framework (5 paragraphs, 5 sentences per paragraph, 5 vocabulary words) is utilized for analytical essays, focusing on clear argumentation, textual evidence, and synthesis of ideas. Regular feedback is provided.

Advanced Vocabulary (LEX A-Z)

Systematic vocabulary acquisition through the LEX curriculum, covering Letters A-Z. Weekly quizzes and integration into written assignments reinforce learning and expand students' lexicons.

Craft Discussions & Literary Analysis

In-depth discussions on literary concepts (e.g., Bildungsroman, satire, magical realism, Shakespearean language, existentialism), rhetorical devices, and essay writing techniques (e.g., thesis statements, finding proof, cause & effect, narration).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-week program guides students through an intensive study of eleven diverse literary works, including novels, plays, and short story collections. Each work is typically covered over 2-3 weeks. Weekly sessions include LEX vocabulary quizzes, 5/5/5 essay reviews and discussions, analysis of themes and literary techniques relevant to the text (e.g., magical realism in "Kafka on the Shore," satire in "Slaughterhouse Five," Shakespearean conventions in "The Tempest"). The course incorporates readings of supplementary texts (e.g., Swift's "A Modest Proposal," Camus' "The Myth of Sisyphus"), in-class essays (e.g., on The Tempest, a mid-term on a chosen story), and a final analytical essay. The course concludes with a discussion of David Foster Wallace's "This is Water."
