The New American Novel: 21st Century Syllabus

Course Overview

This advanced course explores the vibrant landscape of the 21st-century American novel, examining how contemporary authors grapple with enduring questions and new societal challenges. Students will analyze novels that reflect diverse experiences, innovative narrative structures, and pressing themes such as race, identity, historical trauma, technological impact, and the evolution of the "American Dream." The course emphasizes critical literary analysis, engagement with complex ideas, vocabulary development (LEX A-Z), and sophisticated essay writing, particularly the 5/5/5 essay.

Educational Approach

Contemporary Thematic Engagement

Critical discussion of 21st-century issues as reflected in literature, including race and heritage (Whitehead, Diaz, Beatty), gender identity and fluidity (Eugenides), faith and family (Robinson), socio-political commentary (Franzen, Lerner), and apocalyptic/dystopian visions (Ward, McCarthy).

Advanced Analytical & Argumentative Writing

Development of sophisticated writing skills through 5/5/5 essays and analytical responses, focusing on narrative power, character evolution, reliability of narrators, symbolism, and the intersection of literature with contemporary events and ideologies (e.g., analysis of James Baldwin's essays, discussions on "The American Dream").

LEX Vocabulary & Multimedia Integration

Comprehensive vocabulary acquisition using the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) alongside analysis of critical articles (e.g., from The Guardian, New Yorker), interviews (e.g., Colson Whitehead), and discussions of related media like Kendrick Lamar's "To Pimp a Butterfly" to broaden cultural and literary understanding.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course covers ten significant novels from the 21st century, dedicating approximately three weeks to each. Weekly activities include reading assignments (100-125 pages), LEX vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth discussions analyzing themes, characters, and narrative techniques. Writing assignments include 300-400 word responses and 5/5/5 essays. The curriculum integrates primary source documents, critical essays (e.g., by James Baldwin on Black American experience), and multimedia resources (e.g., interviews, music analysis) to contextualize the literature. The course culminates with a final analytical essay on a chosen work or comparative theme.
