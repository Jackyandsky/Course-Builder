The Canon: Close Reading Part XIV - Contemporary American Perspectives

Course Overview

This fourteenth course in "The Canon: Close Reading" series delves into significant works by contemporary American authors from the late 20th and early 21st centuries. Students will engage in intensive textual analysis of fiction and non-fiction that explores complex themes of American identity, societal critique, historical revisionism, race, and the human condition in modern America. The course aims to enhance advanced interpretive skills, engage with diverse narrative styles, and cultivate sophisticated analytical writing through the 5/5/5 essay method and LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Contemporary American Narratives

Close reading of complex narrative structures, including postmodern fragmentation, minimalist prose, historical fiction, and experimental non-fiction. Understanding how contemporary authors reflect and critique American society.

Engagement with Critical Social and Cultural Themes

Exploration of themes such as the American Dream, consumerism, violence, racial identity, historical trauma, addiction, and the search for meaning in contemporary American life. Connecting literary works to current cultural conversations.

Advanced Analytical & Argumentative Essays (5/5/5 Method)

Crafting insightful and rigorously argued analytical essays that engage with the stylistic innovations, thematic complexities, and socio-cultural critiques present in the texts. Continued emphasis on LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected contemporary American authors/works. Each unit involves intensive close reading of assigned texts or substantial excerpts, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and high-level seminar discussions exploring literary techniques and critical interpretations. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, focusing on sophisticated literary analysis and critical engagement with contemporary American issues. The course aims to develop students' abilities to analyze and interpret challenging modern literature with depth and precision.
