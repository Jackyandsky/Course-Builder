The Great American Novel: 20th Century

Course Overview

This course critically examines the concept of "The Great American Novel" through an intensive study of selected 20th-century American literary works often cited as contenders for this elusive title. Students will explore how these novels attempt to capture the essence of the American experience, grappling with themes of national identity, societal aspirations and disillusionments, the complexities of race and class, and the evolving American Dream. The curriculum emphasizes close reading, analysis of modernist and realist techniques, contextual understanding, and crafting sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) with LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Deconstructing "The Great American Novel" Trope

Critical discussion of the very idea of a singular "Great American Novel." Analyzing how each selected work engages with, critiques, or redefines notions of American identity, history, and mythology.

Analysis of American Themes and Literary Styles

Exploring recurring themes such as the pursuit of happiness, individualism vs. community, social mobility, racial injustice, regionalism, and the impact of historical events (e.g., Jazz Age, Great Depression, Civil Rights era). Analyzing modernist and realist narrative techniques.

Advanced Critical Argumentation (5/5/5 Essays)

Crafting insightful analytical essays that evaluate each novel's claim to iconic status, its representation of American life, and its lasting literary significance. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the six selected "Great American Novel" contenders. Each unit involves rigorous close reading, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on how each text embodies or challenges the concept of a definitive American narrative. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, comparing different visions of America and critically engaging with the idea of a national literary epic. The course aims to provide a nuanced understanding of these landmark novels and their complex relationship with American culture and identity.
