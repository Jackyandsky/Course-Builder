Level 10-12 Advanced Reading: The Essential Roth, DeLillo & Franzen

Course Overview

This advanced course for levels 10-12 offers an intensive study of contemporary American literature through the seminal works of Philip Roth, Don DeLillo, and Jonathan Franzen. Students will explore the distinct images of America these authors advance, their unique narrative styles, and their engagement with themes such as Jewish identity, postmodernism, consumerism, family dynamics, and the complexities of modern American life. The course emphasizes critical analysis, vocabulary development, and sophisticated essay writing.

Educational Approach

Author-Specific Thematic Analysis

Deep dives into the characteristic themes of each author: Roth's exploration of Jewish-American identity and historical counter-narratives; DeLillo's postmodern critiques of consumerism, media, and societal anxieties; and Franzen's intricate portrayals of American family life and social commentary.

Advanced Essay Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Students will develop their analytical writing through 5/5/5 essays and other response formats, focusing on constructing complex arguments, integrating textual evidence, and exploring nuanced literary interpretations related to each author's work.

LEX Vocabulary and Critical Reading

Consistent vocabulary enrichment through the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) and critical engagement with supplementary texts, including essays by the authors and articles from publications like The New Yorker, to enhance understanding and analytical precision.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session program is structured around the works of Philip Roth (Weeks 1-9), Don DeLillo (Weeks 10-19), and Jonathan Franzen (Weeks 20-28), covering three major novels from each. Each segment includes reading assignments (100-125 pages weekly), LEX vocabulary quizzes, discussions of themes such as American identity, historical events (e.g., Clinton/Lewinsky affair, Cold War), literary styles (e.g., postmodernism, satire), and the crafting of 300-400 word quick responses as well as more extensive 5/5/5 essays. The course concludes with an analysis of a student-chosen contemporary novel and a final cumulative essay focusing on "The Great American Novel" trope.
