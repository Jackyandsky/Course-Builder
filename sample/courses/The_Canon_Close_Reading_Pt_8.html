The Canon: Close Reading Part VIII - 19th Century Realism & Naturalism

Course Overview

This eighth course in "The Canon: Close Reading" series focuses on the dominant literary movements of the 19th century: Realism and Naturalism. Students will engage in intensive textual analysis of major European novelists who sought to depict everyday life and society with unvarnished truth, often exploring themes of social class, morality, determinism, and the impact of environment on character. The curriculum emphasizes advanced close reading, understanding the philosophical and social underpinnings of these movements, and crafting sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) supported by LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Analysis of Realist and Naturalist Techniques

Close reading to identify and analyze key characteristics of Realism (detailed observation, verisimilitude, focus on ordinary people) and Naturalism (determinism, scientific objectivity, portrayal of harsh realities, influence of heredity and environment).

Social Critique and Character Development

Exploration of how these novelists used their works to critique societal structures, class inequalities, moral hypocrisy, and the human condition. In-depth analysis of complex character psychology and development within their social milieus.

Advanced Interpretive Essays (5/5/5 Method)

Crafting well-supported analytical essays that engage with the philosophical underpinnings of Realism/Naturalism, stylistic features, and thematic concerns of the texts. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives for each of the selected major authors/works (or substantial excerpts), with Austen serving as a point of comparison from early 19th-century realism. Each unit involves rigorous close reading, regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on literary techniques, critical interpretations, and the socio-historical context of 19th-century Europe. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, developing their capacity for nuanced literary criticism. The course aims to provide a thorough understanding of these influential literary movements and their key practitioners.
