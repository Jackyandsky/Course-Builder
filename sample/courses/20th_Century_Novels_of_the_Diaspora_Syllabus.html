Advanced Reading: 20th Century Novels of the Diaspora

Course Overview

This advanced reading course explores influential 20th-century novels focusing on the experiences of diaspora, immigration, exile, and the construction of cultural identity. Students will analyze how authors from various backgrounds depict the complexities of displacement, assimilation, cultural hybridity, intergenerational conflict, and the lingering effects of historical trauma. The course emphasizes critical analysis of literary techniques, thematic exploration, and the development of sophisticated essay writing skills through the 5/5/5 method and LEX vocabulary enrichment.

Educational Approach

Thematic Analysis of Diaspora Experiences

In-depth examination of themes such as identity formation in new cultural contexts, the challenges of assimilation versus cultural preservation, the impact of political and historical events on migration, and the representation of memory and trauma in diasporic narratives.

Structured Analytical Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Development of strong analytical writing skills through the 5/5/5 essay format, focusing on character analysis (e.g., protagonists as "outsiders" or "in-between"), thematic interpretation, and the evaluation of narrative strategies used to convey diasporic experiences.

LEX Vocabulary and Contextual Understanding

Systematic vocabulary building using the complete LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z), integrated with discussions on the historical contexts of migration (e.g., post-colonialism, refugee crises) and critical essays on diaspora literature and cultural theory.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured around five-week intensives for each of the six selected novels. Each unit involves focused reading (approximately 100-125 pages per week), regular LEX vocabulary quizzes (covering letters A-Z cumulatively throughout the course), and detailed class discussions based on "Questions to Ponder" specific to each text. Students will complete multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, exploring character arcs, central conflicts, symbolism, and narrative voice. The course also incorporates supplementary readings, including critical essays on themes of exile and identity, and contextual materials relevant to the specific diasporic communities represented in the literature.
