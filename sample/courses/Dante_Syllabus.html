Dante: An Overview

Course Overview

This course offers an intensive overview of the major works of Dante Alighieri, one of Western literature's most profound poets. Students will journey through *La Vita Nuova* and the three canticles of *The Divine Comedy: Inferno, Purgatorio, and Paradiso*. The curriculum focuses on understanding Dante's complex allegorical vision, his engagement with classical and Christian theology, his innovative use of the Italian vernacular, and the enduring literary and cultural significance of his epic poem. Emphasis is placed on close reading, thematic analysis, contextual understanding, and developing sophisticated analytical essays.

Educational Approach

Allegorical and Symbolic Interpretation

Focus on deciphering the rich layers of allegory and symbolism in Dante's work, connecting them to medieval theology, philosophy (Aristotle, Aquinas), and classical literature (Virgil, Ovid).

Close Reading & Poetic Analysis (Terza Rima)

Detailed analysis of Dante's poetic language, structure (particularly terza rima), imagery, and characterization. Students will explore how these elements contribute to the overall meaning and impact of the works.

5/5/5 Analytical Essays & LEX Vocabulary

Development of advanced analytical writing skills through 5/5/5 essays on themes such as divine justice, human sin, redemption, and the nature of love. Comprehensive vocabulary building with LEX Advanced (Letters A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured into five-week intensives covering *La Vita Nuova* and each canticle of *The Divine Comedy*. Each unit involves close reading of assigned cantos/sections (approximately 100-125 pages of prose equivalent per week), regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth discussions on "Questions to Ponder" related to plot, character, symbolism, and theological concepts. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per work, exploring complex themes and Dante's literary artistry. The course includes primers on medieval Florence, classical influences (Virgil), and key theological concepts to provide essential context.
