Advanced Placement European History

Course Overview

This Advanced Placement European History course provides a college-level survey of European history from approximately 1450 to the present. Students will explore the interactions of European states and societies that played a significant role in the continent’s development and its global impact. The course emphasizes the development of historical thinking skills, including analyzing primary and secondary sources, making historical comparisons, understanding causation, and identifying patterns of continuity and change over time. The curriculum is designed to prepare students thoroughly for the AP European History exam.

Key Historical Periods & Thematic Focus

Renaissance & Exploration (c. 1450 – c. 1648)

Revival of classical learning, artistic innovations, religious reformations, global exploration, and the rise of new monarchies. Focus on humanism, the Protestant Reformation, and early colonial expansion.

Absolutism & Enlightenment (c. 1648 – c. 1815)

Development of absolutist states, constitutional monarchies, the Scientific Revolution, and the Enlightenment's impact on political and social thought, leading to revolutionary movements.

Industrialization & 19th Century Upheaval (c. 1815 – c. 1914)

The Industrial Revolution's impact on society, economy, and politics; rise of new ideologies (liberalism, conservatism, socialism, nationalism); unification movements; and growing imperialism.

20th Century Conflicts & Cold War (c. 1914 – Present)

World War I, Russian Revolution, rise of totalitarianism, World War II, the Holocaust, the Cold War, decolonization, European integration, and contemporary challenges.

Interaction of Europe and the World

Analyzing Europe's global influence through colonialism, trade, cultural exchange, and conflict, and how global events impacted Europe.

Poverty and Prosperity

Examining economic developments, including agricultural changes, industrialization, capitalism, socialism, and efforts to address poverty and inequality.

Educational Approach

Historical Thinking Skills

Emphasis on developing skills such as analyzing historical causation, patterns of continuity and change, comparison, contextualization, and argumentation using historical evidence from primary and secondary sources.

Source Analysis (Primary & Secondary)

Regular practice in analyzing a variety of historical sources, including written documents, images, maps, and quantitative data, to understand different perspectives and construct historical interpretations.

AP Exam Preparation: DBQ & LEQ Writing

Focused instruction and practice on writing Document-Based Questions (DBQs) and Long Essay Questions (LEQs), including developing strong theses, using evidence effectively, and understanding scoring guidelines. Multiple-choice question strategies are also covered.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is structured chronologically and thematically, covering the key periods and themes of European history as outlined by the AP curriculum. Each unit involves lectures, discussions, analysis of primary and secondary source readings (e.g., from McKay's "A History of Western Society"), and practice with AP-style questions. Students will complete multiple DBQs and LEQs throughout the course, receiving detailed feedback to improve their historical argumentation and writing skills. The curriculum aims for a comprehensive understanding of European history and thorough preparation for all components of the AP exam.
