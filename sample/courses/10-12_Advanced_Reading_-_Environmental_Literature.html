Level 10-12 Advanced Reading: Environmentalism in Literature

Course Overview

This course offers an advanced exploration of environmentalism as a critical theme in literature. Students will engage with seminal works that articulate the complex relationship between humanity and the natural world, analyze the historical development of the environmental movement (from Rachel Carson's "The Silent Spring" to modern concerns like global warming), and discuss various forms of environmental activism and potential solutions to impending ecological challenges. The curriculum is designed to foster critical thinking, analytical writing, and vocabulary enrichment through literary study.

Educational Approach

Analytical Essay Writing (5/5/5 Method)

Students will engage in rigorous analytical writing, often using a 5/5/5 essay structure, to explore themes such as environmental activism, pessimism vs. optimism in human-nature relationships, and the symbolic role of nature in literature.

Critical Discussion and Debate

Class sessions will involve in-depth discussions on topics like the efficacy of different forms of environmental activism, the literary portrayal of ecological catastrophe, and the philosophical underpinnings of environmental literature, supported by readings of supplementary articles and short stories.

LEX Vocabulary Enrichment

Systematic vocabulary development through LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) and contextual words from texts, reinforced by regular quizzes to enhance descriptive and analytical language skills.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session program is designed around an intensive study of ten key literary works focusing on environmental themes, beginning with Richard Powers' "The Overstory" and culminating with a student-selected novel for an end-of-year assessment. Each novel is typically covered over 2-3 weeks, involving reading assignments (100-150 pages weekly), vocabulary quizzes, and 5/5/5 analytical essays. The curriculum integrates discussions on relevant articles (e.g., exploring Richard Powers's influences, the impact of Thoreau's "Walden"), short stories (e.g., by Doris Lessing, Isabel Allende, Shinichi Hoshi, Ray Bradbury), and critical analysis of concepts such as existentialism and the portrayal of apocalypse in literature. A mid-term quiz focuses on analytical essay writing based on a selected text.
