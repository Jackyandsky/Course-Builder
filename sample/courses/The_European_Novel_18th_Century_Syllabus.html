The European Novel: 18th Century

Course Overview

This course surveys the development of the novel across Europe during the 18th century, a period of significant literary innovation and intellectual ferment. Students will examine key works from various national traditions, including French, German, and English literature, focusing on how the novel engaged with Enlightenment ideals, explored themes of reason and sentiment, and experimented with narrative forms. The curriculum emphasizes comparative analysis, understanding diverse cultural contexts, close reading, and crafting sophisticated analytical essays (5/5/5 method) with LEX Advanced vocabulary.

Educational Approach

Comparative Analysis of European Novelistic Traditions

Examining the distinct characteristics and shared concerns of the emerging novel in different European countries, focusing on how cultural and philosophical contexts shaped literary production.

Enlightenment Philosophy in Literature

Analyzing how 18th-century novels reflected, debated, and disseminated Enlightenment ideas about reason, individual liberty, social contracts, education, and human nature. Exploring the tension between reason and sentiment.

Study of Narrative Forms (Epistolary, Philosophical Tale)

Understanding the diverse narrative forms popular in the 18th century, including the epistolary novel, the philosophical tale, the sentimental novel, and early realist tendencies. Continued mastery of LEX Advanced vocabulary (A-Z).

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session course is organized into five-week intensives, each focusing on one or two key authors/works or a national/thematic tradition from 18th-century European literature. Each unit involves rigorous close reading of assigned texts (or substantial excerpts), regular LEX Advanced vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth seminar discussions on narrative innovations, thematic explorations, and the intellectual climate of the Enlightenment. Students will write multiple 5/5/5 analytical essays per unit, developing their skills in comparative literary analysis and historical contextualization. The course aims to provide a broad yet nuanced understanding of the European novel's formative period.
