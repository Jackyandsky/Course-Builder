Level 10-12 Advanced Reading: 19th Century Literature

Course Overview

This advanced course for levels 10-12 provides an in-depth survey of influential literature from the 19th century. Students will explore canonical works by authors such as Wilde, Stoker, Hawthorne, Brontë, Dickens, Crane, Gaskell, Dostoevsky, and Conrad. The course emphasizes understanding these texts within their historical context, including the legacy of the French Revolution, shifting social ideas, class structures, and the rise of literary movements like Romanticism, Gothic fiction, and Realism. Critical analysis, thematic exploration, and vocabulary development are central to the curriculum.

Educational Approach

Contextual Literary Analysis

Students will analyze texts by considering 19th-century historical trends, social issues (e.g., Puritan society, Industrial Revolution, Victorian values, colonialism), and literary movements. Emphasis is placed on art, ethics, morality, feminism, and the Bildungsroman.

5/5/5 Essay Writing & Critical Responses

Development of analytical writing through the 5/5/5 essay format and other written responses. Students will address complex thematic questions, compare characters, and analyze literary techniques like irony and symbolism.

LEX Vocabulary and Poetic Analysis

Comprehensive vocabulary building using the LEX curriculum (Letters A-Z) and contextual words from the texts. The course also includes analysis of 19th-century poetry, focusing on poetic elements and themes relevant to the era.

Curriculum Structure

The 30-session program is organized into thematic units, each focusing on 1-2 major novels over approximately 2-3 weeks per novel, totaling ten core texts. Weekly sessions involve reading (average 100-150 pages), LEX vocabulary quizzes, and in-depth discussions of "Questions to Ponder" related to themes, characterization (e.g., anti-heroes, heroines), and literary devices. Students will complete 5/5/5 essays, shorter research responses (e.g., on the Faust myth, 19th C. feminism), and engage with supplementary materials such as articles on literary criticism (e.g., "Crane’s Realistic Treatment of War"), historical context (e.g., "The Industrial Revolution"), and poetry packages (Tennyson, Arnold, Rossettis, Brownings, Kipling, Keats). The course culminates in a final essay analyzing a chosen short story, poem, or essay from the period.
